Skype username: BritishEnglish_Teacher Different Ways to Talk About the Time One / One o'clock One in the morning One o'clock in the morning Three o'clock in the morning Three in the afternoon 1 a.m. 1 p.m. 0100 1300 1 a.m. = 0100 1 p.m. = 1300 24-Hour Clock 0000 - 2359 0000 = midnight 1200 = midday 0000 -- 1200 = morning 1200 -- 1800 = afternoon 1800 -- 2100 = evening 2100 -- 0600 = night 1315 = "one-fifteen (in the afternoon)" / quarter past one 1420 = twenty (minutes) past two / two twenty 1830 = half past six / half six / six-thirty 1945 = "seven forty-five (in the evening)" / quarter to eight 2050 = "eight fifty (in the evening)" / ten to nine Prepositions Use "by" to describe a time that something has to be done by, is usually done by, or a point by which something was done: "You must clean your room by tea time." "You will have your answer by midday." Use "at" to specify what time an action is done / will be done / was done at: "He will catch the train at 1000." "I usually hit the gym at seven in the evening." "They left at nine this morning." Use "in", "during", "through", "throughout" and "over" to discuss periods of times for actions: "She worked in / during / throughout the summer." "To enjoy the good times, you have to keep going through the bad." "The football player performed very well over a number of weeks." Use "in" to specify how long until something happens in the future: "She'll be back in a few minutes." "My current contract will end in four months' time." Use "ago" to explain how long it is since something happened in the past: "My grandfather died more than 20 years ago." "He only left about ten minutes ago." Use "since" to describe a period of time in the past up until the present or another specified point of time (use with present perfect or past perfect tense): "I've been in my present job since 2005." "At the time of his death, he'd been suffering from the illness since he was diagnosed three years before." "We haven't seen each other since we broke up four months ago." "I haven't gone out since last Thursday." If you want someone to wait for you, here are some expressions you can try: Just a second / moment / minute Hang on (one second / moment) Hold on One second / moment / minute I'll be right with you (formal)
